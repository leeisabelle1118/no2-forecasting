# NO₂ Forecasting — Graphs & Visualizations Guide

This guide explains every graph produced across notebooks 01–03, what each one
shows, and why it matters for predicting NO₂. The notebooks form a deliberate
pipeline: explore raw data → understand temporal structure → diagnose trained
models.

---

## The Big Picture: How the Notebooks Connect

```text
Notebook 01  →  Notebook 02  →  Notebook 03
(Data Quality   (Temporal        (Model
 & Spatial       Structure)       Diagnostics)
 Exploration)
     ↓               ↓               ↓
 Know WHAT        Know WHEN       Know WHERE
 data you have    patterns exist  the model fails
```

A forecasting model trained on data it does not understand will not generalize.
Notebooks 01 and 02 build that understanding. Notebook 03 checks whether the
trained models actually captured what EDA revealed.

## Time Convention (Important)

- All timestamps are treated as **UTC** in these plots.
- Stations are **not timezone-normalized** to local clock time.
- Daylight saving transitions and longitude differences therefore shift how local
  rush-hour behavior appears on a shared UTC axis.
- Interpret hour-of-day peaks as **network-level UTC structure**, not a single
  local-time profile.

## Notebook 01 — `01_explore_airnow.ipynb`
### Exploratory Data Analysis

This notebook answers foundational questions before training: Is the data usable?
Where are the sensors? What does a typical day look like?

---

### 1. Missing Data Heatmap
**File:** `outputs/eda_missing_data_heatmap.png`

![Histogram of site-level missingness and line chart of daily network missing rate](outputs/eda_missing_data_heatmap.png)

Two side-by-side panels summarize data reliability.

- **Left: site-level missing-data histogram**
  - Bars show how many stations are missing a given fraction of hourly readings.
  - Left-skewed concentration near 0% implies most stations are reliable.
  - A right tail indicates sparse sites that may need exclusion or heavy imputation.

- **Right: daily network missing-rate line**
  - Daily fraction of stations with missing readings.
  - Low/flat values suggest independent random gaps.
  - Spikes suggest shared outages (weather, maintenance, ingestion interruptions).

**Why it matters:**
Missingness directly affects sequence quality. With a 24-hour look-back, long gaps
can corrupt model inputs. The observed overall missing rate (~13.7% of station-hour
entries before site filtering) supported short-gap interpolation (≤3 hours) rather
than discarding all affected windows.

**Interpretation pitfall:**
A low global missing rate can still hide a few stations with severe sparsity.

---

### 2. Site Map
**File:** `outputs/eda_site_map.png`

Scatter of ~197 stations by latitude/longitude. Color encodes each station’s
time-averaged NO₂ level (yellow low, orange/red high).

**What it shows:**
- Spatial coverage and sensor density.
- Urban-rural contrast in baseline NO₂.
- Uneven regional sampling (sparser west vs denser east in this dataset slice).

**Why it matters:**
A pooled model across heterogeneous sites must handle large baseline differences.
Per-station normalization (divide by training-set mean) prevents high-baseline
urban sites from dominating optimization.

**Interpretation pitfall:**
A station’s mean level does not capture its event-driven volatility.

---

### 3. NO₂ Time Series: Representative Sites
**File:** `outputs/eda_no2_time_series.png`

![Three full-period hourly NO₂ traces for highest-mean, lowest-mean, and highest-coverage stations](outputs/eda_no2_time_series.png)

Three full-period hourly traces (Jul 2023–Sep 2024):
- Highest-mean site
- Lowest-mean site
- Highest-coverage site

**Why it matters:**
This is a pre-training sanity check: temporal structure is visible, dynamic range
is realistic, and gaps are represented as missingness rather than silent artifacts.

**Interpretation pitfall:**
Representative sites are illustrative, not exhaustive of all behaviors.

---

### 4. Diurnal Cycle
**File:** `outputs/eda_diurnal_cycle.png`

![Mean NO₂ by UTC hour with 10th–90th percentile station spread band](outputs/eda_diurnal_cycle.png)

X-axis: UTC hour (0–23). Y-axis: mean NO₂ (ppb). The line is the overall mean;
the shaded band is the 10th–90th percentile across stations.

**What it shows:**
- Morning enhancement
- Afternoon photochemical dip
- Modest evening rise
- Overnight minimum

**Why it matters:**
The diurnal cycle is the dominant short-term signal. A 24-hour look-back contains
one full cycle; models should reproduce this structure to generalize well.

**Interpretation pitfall:**
A shared UTC hour corresponds to different local clock times across longitudes.

---

### 5. Monthly Seasonal Pattern (Box Plot)
**File:** `plots/monthly_boxplot.png`

![Boxplots of station-level monthly mean NO₂ for each calendar month](plots/monthly_boxplot.png)

One box per month, built from station-level monthly means:
- Center line: median
- Box: IQR
- Whiskers: 1.5 × IQR
- Points: outlier stations

**What it shows:**
Higher winter central tendency, lower summer central tendency, and potential
month-dependent spread changes.

**Why it matters:**
Month-of-year strongly modulates ambient NO₂. The 15-month window includes a full
annual cycle, which is critical for learning seasonal transitions.

**Interpretation pitfall:**
Outlier markers are distributional outliers, not automatically “bad sensors.”

---

### 6. Daily Mean NO₂: All Sites
**File:** `outputs/eda_daily_mean_all_sites.png`

![Network daily mean NO₂ with shaded ±1 standard deviation across stations](outputs/eda_daily_mean_all_sites.png)

Daily network mean with shaded ±1 SD across sites.

**What it shows:**
- Broad multi-month trend
- High-variance dates (wide band)
- More homogeneous dates (narrow band)

**Why it matters:**
Large departures often correspond to difficult forecast periods. This plot sets
context for expected performance variation over time.

### 7. Daily Mean NO₂: Single Site
**File:** `outputs/eda_daily_mean_site.png`

![Daily mean NO₂ time series for station 081030006](outputs/eda_daily_mean_site.png)

Daily mean trajectory for one station (`081030006`).

Twelve lines (one per month), each showing mean NO₂ by UTC hour.

### 4. Rolling Average / Smoothed Trends
**File:** `plots/ts_rolling_avg.png`

![Raw daily mean with 7-day and 30-day rolling averages](plots/ts_rolling_avg.png)

- Light gray: raw daily mean
- Blue: 7-day rolling mean
- Dark red: 30-day rolling mean

**Why it matters:**
Separates short- and medium-scale structure. Remaining residual correlation at
short lags can later appear in diagnostic ACF/PACF.

### 5. Anomaly Bar Chart
**File:** `plots/ts_anomaly.png`

![Daily anomaly bars vs 30-day baseline with high/low IQR thresholds](plots/ts_anomaly.png)

**Why it matters:**
Identifies extreme periods that typically drive disproportionate forecast error.

**Interpretation pitfall:**
IQR thresholds are heuristic anomaly flags, not labeled causal events.

## Notebook 03 — `03_model_diagnostics.ipynb`
### Model Diagnostics

Notebook 03 tests whether models learned the structure established in EDA.

---

### 1. Forecast Error Time Decomposition
**File:** `plots/diag_error_decomp.png`

Two grouped bar charts for Transformer (blue) and Mamba (orange):
- MAE by first predicted UTC hour
- MAE by month of year

**Why it matters:**
Shows when each model is weakest and whether error patterns are consistent with
known high-variance periods from EDA.

**Interpretation note:**
Temporal alignment between high-variance periods and high MAE is suggestive, not
by itself proof of causality.

---

### 2. Attention Weight Heatmap
**File:** `plots/diag_attention_weights.png`

Heatmaps (per Transformer layer) over 24×24 query-key positions, averaged across
sampled test windows.

Useful motifs:
- Bright diagonal: local lag dependence
- Bright right edge: recency bias
- Bright lag-24 region: daily-cycle reuse
- Diffuse weights: weak specialization

**Why it matters:**
Offers direct interpretability on what temporal lags the model uses.

**Interpretation pitfall:**
Attention highlights association, not guaranteed causal contribution.

---

### 3. Residual Autocorrelation — ACF & PACF
**File:** `plots/diag_residual_acf.png`

2×2 grid:
- Transformer ACF/PACF
- Mamba ACF/PACF

Residual series is per-window mean signed error:
(predicted − actual) averaged over forecast steps and sites, yielding one value
per test window.

**Why it matters:**
Significant spikes outside the 95% CI indicate remaining temporal structure the
model did not absorb.

Common readings:
- Lag 1 spike: short-term underreaction
- Lag 24 spike: incomplete daily-cycle modeling
- Lag 1–6 cluster: residual short-memory pattern

**Statistical caution:**
When checking many lags, some spikes may appear by chance (multiple comparisons).
Interpret persistent, coherent patterns more strongly than isolated single spikes.

## Known Limitations

- Spatial coverage is not uniform across the U.S. domain represented here.
- The training window spans ~15 months (one annual cycle), limiting robustness
  for interannual variability.
- NO₂-only forecasting can miss explanatory covariates (meteorology, smoke,
  weekday/holiday regime shifts).
- IQR-based anomaly definitions are heuristic and not event-labeled ground truth.
